Safe foods — budgies
✓Vegetables: leafy greens (kale, spinach, romaine), carrot, broccoli, peas, corn, bell pepper, zucchini, cucumber, sweet potato (cooked or raw). Chop or grate small.
✓Fruits: apple (no seeds), banana, mango, blueberry, strawberry, melon, pear, peach (no pit). Small amounts only — high sugar.
✓Cooked grains: brown rice, quinoa, plain oats. Small amount as occasional treat.
✓Cooked egg: plain scrambled or hard-boiled, tiny pinch. High protein treat — not daily.
✓Fresh herbs: basil, parsley, cilantro, dill — budgies enjoy picking at fresh herb sprigs.
Never feed — budgies
✗Avocado — toxic, can be fatal even in small amounts.
✗Onion and garlic — toxic, damages red blood cells.
✗Chocolate, caffeine, alcohol — all toxic.
✗Raisins and grapes in large amounts — raisins especially; Kaylor already contains them so no additional raisins anywhere.
✗Fruit seeds and pits — apple seeds, cherry/peach/plum pits contain cyanide compounds.
✗Rhubarb, raw beans, xylitol, mushrooms, salty or heavily seasoned food.
✗Iceberg lettuce — no nutritional value, causes loose droppings. Use romaine or leafy greens instead.
Harrison's High Potency Super Fine shelf life: 45 days once the bag is opened. Write the open date on the bag. Buy 1 lb bags only — larger bags will go stale before you use them.
Out-of-cage & free-flight time — Billy, Morgan, Doc & Wyatt
Budgies need flight to stay healthy. A budgie that never leaves the cage develops muscle atrophy, weight gain, and boredom-driven feather destructive behaviors. Aim for at least 1–2 hours of supervised free-flight daily.
✓Before letting them out: ceiling fans off, windows and doors closed, Clarence out of the room, cats or other pets secured. Check that no toxic plants are accessible (Pothos, Philodendron, Fiddle Leaf Fig, Alocasia, Anthurium are the priorities).
✓Place the play gym adjacent to the cage opening so they can hop or fly directly onto it — budgies are reluctant to cross open air to reach a gym. Scatter a pinch of dry mix or oats on the platform to give them a reason to land.
✓All four out together is ideal — flock behavior means if one flies somewhere, the others follow. They are much more confident as a group than individually.
✓Return to cage: Never chase. Dim the room slightly or turn off lights briefly — budgies naturally return to their cage (the safe, bright, food-filled space) when the environment changes. Offering millet inside the cage also works reliably.
✗Never leave free-flying budgies unsupervised. Mirrors, windows, and ceiling fans are the three biggest hazards during flight time.
✗Don't let them fly near Maria or Lou Dog's cage unsupervised — GCCs can and do injure budgies through cage bars.

Why these cages — compatibility reasoning
Cage 1 — Bean, Polly, Vinnie & Fabbie: Owl finches and society/spice finches are the closest temperament match in your flock — both gentle, non-aggressive species. Society finches have actually hybridized with owl finches in nature, confirming genetic and behavioral compatibility. The spice finch component in Vinnie and Fabbie adds no aggression. One male (Vinnie) with three females requires monitoring but is workable.
Cage 2 — Bonnie, Clyde, Donut & Ocean: All four zebra finches together is the cleanest pairing. Same species means they understand each other's social signals and hierarchy. Two established pairs (Bonnie & Clyde, Donut & Ocean) create natural balance. Zebra finches can be pushy with other species but are generally fine with their own kind at this group size.
Why NOT zebra finches with owl finches: Zebra finches are classified as "bullies" in finch compatibility guides. Owl finches are mellow and don't defend themselves well against more assertive birds. Keeping Bean and Polly with Bonnie, Clyde, Donut, and Ocean would put the owl finches under ongoing stress.

Training treats — what actually works for Lou Dog
Lou Dog goes wild for food you're eating — that's classic GCC flock behavior. In the wild, eating is social: if you're eating it, it must be valuable. Use this as your training lever.
★Pomegranate seeds — the #1 GCC obsession in the community. Messy, intensely flavored, irresistible. Small enough for single-reward bites. Nutritious. Try these first.
★Pine nuts — tiny, high-fat, extremely motivating. One pine nut per successful behavior is a classic professional bird trainer technique. The fat content makes them genuinely high-value in a bird's brain.
★Almond sliver — not a whole almond, literally a thin sliver. Same high-fat high-value principle. Unsalted, unroasted only.
★Dried bird pepper / chili tip — capsaicin doesn't affect birds but the texture and aroma are very appealing to GCCs. A small piece of dried bird pepper or fresh chili tip.
★Eat with him first — before a training session, sit with something he can have a piece of (apple, banana, grape) and let him eat from your hand. He'll enter "food mode." Then transition to training with small pieces of that same food as reward. You're borrowing the motivation from the social eating context.
✓Enthusiastic praise — may be his highest-value reward of all. Some GCCs work as hard for social excitement from their person as for food. Test both and see what makes him more animated.

Training session rules: 5–10 min max, right before his normal mealtime when hungriest. Have 10–15 tiny treat pieces ready. Reward arrives within 2 seconds of the behavior — timing is everything. Start with step-up (he likely knows it), get fluent, then build new behaviors from there.

Play gym engagement
Lou Dog is social first, forager second. He needs a reason to stay on the gym, not just land on it.
✓Hang a foraging toy from the gym — something he has to work to get a treat out of. Pomegranate seed or pine nut inside.
✓A foot toy on the platform he can pick up and manipulate — cabone ring, cork piece, wood bead on macrame.
✓Scatter plain rolled oats (raw, unflavored) on the platform surface — safe, creates a foraging activity, and he'll pick through them.
✓Designate the gym as your training perch — do all training sessions there. The gym becomes associated with good things and he'll want to go back.
✓Be near or on the gym yourself — your presence is the biggest draw. He'll follow you to it.
Training progression — start here, build in order
Sessions: 5–10 min max, before mealtime, reward within 2 seconds. End every session on a success — even an easy one. Never end frustrated.
1. Target training — the foundation of everything
Hold a wooden chopstick or dowel near Lou Dog. The moment his beak touches it, reward instantly. Once reliable, move the stick to different positions so he has to follow it. Target training is the base skill that makes every other trick easier to teach — it teaches him that touching something on cue = treat. Takes 1–3 sessions for most GCCs. Resource: Parrot Wizard on YouTube has a GCC-specific first target training video that walks through this exactly.
2. Step up on cue (verbal)
He likely does this already, but the goal is a reliable verbal cue — say "step up," present finger, reward every time even if he was already going to do it. Never skip the reward. Once reliable, add "step down" to return to perch on cue. These two commands alone make every vet visit, cage return, and safety situation much easier.
3. Wave
Say "wave" and offer your finger for a step-up — but pull it away just before he grabs it. His foot will stay lifted for a moment. That moment is your reward window. Click or say "yes" and treat immediately. Repeat. After several sessions the word alone will lift the foot. This builds directly from step-up so teach that first.
4. Turn around / spin
Hold a treat to his side so he has to turn his head to follow it. Lure him in a full circle rewarding at completion. Add the verbal "spin" once he's doing the circle reliably. GCCs typically pick this up very fast — it's one of their most natural trick behaviors.
5. Recall (fly to me)
Once Lou Dog is comfortable flying during out-of-cage time, recall training is the most useful safety skill you can teach. Start with short distances on a play gym, say his name + "come," reward the moment he lands on you. Build distance slowly. A reliable recall can prevent an escape situation from becoming a crisis.
6. Fetch
GCCs can learn to retrieve small objects. Start by rewarding him for picking up a small foot toy. Then reward for carrying it toward you. Then reward only for dropping it in your hand. Takes patience but once learned is endlessly entertaining for both of you.
7. Color/shape identification (advanced)
GCCs are intelligent enough to learn to pick a specific colored object from a group on cue. Use colored foot toys or caps. Say "red" and reward only when he picks the red one. This takes weeks of consistent sessions but is a genuine cognitive exercise.
Games — interactive play that isn't formal training
🏀 Basketball
Small ball, small hoop (conure-sized sets exist on Amazon and Etsy). Lure him to drop the ball through the hoop using target training. GCCs often become obsessed with this. Can find mini basketball sets specifically for small parrots on Amazon for under $10.
🛒 Shopping cart / skateboard pushing
Miniature wooden shopping carts and skateboards made for parrots teach object manipulation. He pushes it with his beak or feet for a treat. The Wontee Bird Toys Mini Shopping Cart set (Amazon, ~$10) is specifically sized for conures and cockatiels and includes the cart, skateboard, and rings.
🧩 Cup stacking / ring on peg
Small stacking cups or a ring-on-peg toy teaches sequential problem solving. He learns to put the ring on the post for a reward. Start with just one ring and build up. More of a shaping project than a quick game — very rewarding when he gets it.
📦 Box opening
Put a pomegranate seed or pine nut inside a small cardboard box with a lid. Let him work out how to open it. No training needed — the motivation is built in. Escalate to boxes with flaps, then boxes inside boxes. Costs nothing and keeps him busy for 10–20 minutes.
💬 Talking and mimicry
GCCs have a limited but real vocabulary with consistent practice. Repeat simple words in context — "hello" when you approach, "step up" during training, his name before treats. Don't expect African Grey-level speech but many GCCs learn 5–15 words and phrases. Talk to him constantly during out-of-cage time.
🕵 Hide and seek
Go around a corner and call his name. When he flies or walks to find you, big reward and praise. Builds recall and uses his natural flock-finding instinct. Only works if he's confident in the space — don't do this if he's anxious about being separated from you.

Minimum 2–4 hours daily, supervised. Welfare requirement — not optional enrichment. Conures kept mostly caged develop screaming, feather plucking, biting, depression.

Sleeping setup
✓Rope boing (recommended) — a coiled cotton-only rope perch hung from the top of the cage. GCCs love them because they can grip all the way around and the slight sway is comforting. He will likely adopt it as his permanent sleeping spot within a few days. Inspect weekly for fraying — replace when strands unravel. Cotton only, never jute or synthetic.
✓High wooden perch — a natural wood perch (willow, apple, or manzanita) positioned at the highest point of the cage also works perfectly. Birds instinctively feel safest sleeping at height.
✗No fabric huts, sleep tents, fuzzy pouches, or any enclosed soft structure — fiber ingestion risk and hormonal behavior trigger regardless of sex.
⚠ Nonstick cookware (Teflon/PTFE) fumes kill birds within minutes. Colorless, odorless. Switch to stainless, cast iron, or ceramic. Applies to entire home always.
⚠ No aerosols, scented candles, air fresheners, plug-in diffusers, or incense near any bird cage.

Chronic egg laying — prevention protocol
1.Pellets first, always. Never let Maria drift back to a seed-heavy diet. This is the single most important protection against egg-laying complications.
2.Head and neck only when petting Maria. Stroking her back, wings, or vent area stimulates mating behavior and directly triggers hormonal egg-laying. Non-negotiable.
3.Limit daylight to 10–12 hours. Cover her cage earlier in the evening. Long days trigger reproductive cycles.
4.No nesting materials, no dark enclosed spaces, no fabric huts, no mirrors. All of these trigger egg-laying. A high perch is her sleeping spot.
5.If she lays an egg: don't remove it immediately. Let her sit on it 2–3 weeks. Early removal causes replacement. Swap for a fake egg if available — she'll eventually abandon it.
6.Egg binding emergency: straining, tail bobbing with each breath, sitting on floor, visible distress. Same-day vet emergency — do not wait.

Enrichment — what Maria enjoys
🔔Bells — her #1 obsession. Stainless steel only, remove clappers. Place at her height inside the cage and on the play gym. She will ring it and sing at it constantly.
🎵Music — the single best enrichment for a cockatiel. Play music or whistle to her during the day. She will mimic melodies and household sounds. Female cockatiels vocalize less than males but engage enthusiastically with sound.
🌾Simple foraging — cockatiels prefer simple over complex. Millet clipped to bars, Nutriberry to work on, small paper foraging wrap with seed inside. She doesn't need puzzle toys — basic foraging activities are more engaging for her than complex puzzles.
🪜Swings — heavy use expected. Place in the upper area of the cage. Moving perch objects are a cockatiel favorite.
🪵Light wood chews — willow and balsa pieces. Good for beak health and satisfies the urge to chew without the destruction level of a conure.
🌾Oats and spice blend on the play gym platform — scatter raw rolled oats and a pinch of her bird spice blend. Cockatiels are ground foragers by nature — picking through material on a flat surface is instinctively compelling.
✗No mirrors for Maria — ever. Female cockatiels bond to their reflection and treat it as a mate, directly triggering hormonal egg-laying cycles.

Universal toy safety rules — all birds
✗No zinc or lead — galvanized metal, cheap chain links, mystery alloys. Zinc toxicity is a common and serious bird emergency.
✗No jute rope — frays into long strands that wrap around toes and necks.
✗No synthetic fibers — polyester, nylon, acrylic. Cotton only for rope/fiber elements.
✗No walnut wood — contains juglone, toxic to birds.
✗No painted, dyed, or varnished wood — natural and untreated only. Unfinished wood beads only.
✗No glue — adhesives in toy assembly can be toxic when chewed.
✗No craft store feathers — treated with preservatives, disease risk from unknown birds.
✗No burlap — jute-based, treated with mineral oils, sheds aggressively, toe entanglement risk.
✓Inspect all toys weekly. Replace anything with fraying rope, broken parts, or sharp chewed edges.
✓Safe materials: untreated natural wood (balsa, pine, willow, birch, apple, basswood, sola/tapioca), seagrass, vine, corn husk, palm, unbleached paper, stainless steel, 100% cotton rope, vegetable-tanned leather.
✓Rotate 2 toys per budgie cage every Friday. Lou Dog: new or rotated toys weekly or when destroyed.

Household Plants — Bird Safety Guide

All 20 plants in the Grossman home, rated for the flock. Applies to all birds: Billy, Morgan, Doc, Wyatt, Bonnie, Clyde, Donut, Ocean, Bean, Polly, Vinnie, Fabbie, Lou Dog, and Maria.

⚠ Even plants rated "safe" can cause issues if chewed heavily or if a bird accesses soil or fertilizer. Supervise all birds near plants during out-of-cage time. Never use chemical pesticides near any bird. Neem oil is safer but use with birds out of the room and isolate treated plants 24–48 hours.

🚫 Toxic to birds — keep out of all free-flight areas

These plants cause real harm if chewed or contacted — calcium oxalate crystals, oral pain, swelling
🌿 Alocasia / Elephant Ear — white geometric pot
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Calcium oxalate crystals cause immediate oral pain and swelling. One of the highest-concern plants in the home. Keep completely out of budgie and Lou Dog/Maria free-flight areas. Never allow any bird near this plant unsupervised.
🌿 Anthurium — glass jar of water
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Calcium oxalate crystals. Growing in water makes it extra accessible — birds may try to drink from the jar. Keep off all surfaces accessible during out-of-cage time. Cold sensitive below 50°F.
🌿 Pothos (gray pot, cascading)
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Calcium oxalate crystals. Cascading growth habit means trailing vines can reach floor level — especially dangerous during budgie or GCC out-of-cage time. Consider moving outside permanently where it would thrive on a fence or wall.
🌿 Pothos (copper/gold pot on stand by window)
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Same species as gray pot pothos — calcium oxalate crystals. Window placement means birds may investigate during flight. Same outdoor option applies — could move outside permanently.
🌿 Philodendron — kitchen counter near books
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Calcium oxalate crystals — higher concern plant. Kitchen counter placement is especially risky. Budgies and Lou Dog must NOT have kitchen access during out-of-cage time while this plant is there. Strongly consider moving outside — does great in zone 9b.
🌳 Fiddle Leaf Fig — floor plant (tall)
🚫 Toxic to ALL birds 🚫 Toxic to Clarence
Calcium oxalate crystals in leaves and stems — same mechanism as Pothos and Philodendron, not just irritating sap as originally rated. Floor placement makes it highly accessible during out-of-cage time. Lou Dog as an active chewer is at the highest risk. Keep birds away during free-flight.

Non-negotiables — universal rules

Household toxins — all birds at all times
✗Nonstick cookware (Teflon/PTFE) — fumes colorless, odorless, kill birds within minutes. Switch to stainless, cast iron, or ceramic. Applies to entire home, always.
✗Aerosol sprays, scented candles, air fresheners, plug-in diffusers, incense — toxic to all bird respiratory systems. Maria and Lou Dog most sensitive but applies to all cages.
✗Ceiling fans — off whenever any bird is out of cage. Fatal injury risk.
✗Clarence unsupervised with free-roaming birds — ever.
No fabric huts or soft sleeping tents — ever
✗Lou Dog: hormonal behavior trigger + fiber ingestion risk (fatal crop impaction). Rope boing or high perch only.
✗Maria: directly triggers egg-laying cycles. Hard no. High perch only for sleeping.
✗Budgies: fiber ingestion risk. Wooden box with entrance hole is the safe enclosed-sleep alternative if needed.
Food rules
✗Grit — never for any bird. Budgies, finches, cockatiels, and conures all hull seeds before swallowing. Grit causes crop impaction. Cuttlebone covers calcium.
✗Egg food in batch jar — moisture shortens shelf life. Separate side dish only, Mon/Wed/Fri, remove after 2–3 hours.
✗Egg food daily — triggers hormonal and breeding behavior in all species.
✗Millet as a staple — standalone millet spray 1x/week max per budgie cage.
✓Freeze-dried ingredients: one-item ingredient list only. No sulfites, added sugar, salt, or natural flavors. No raisins anywhere.
Dish hygiene
✓Blow seed hulls daily — a full-looking dish may be 90% empty shells.
✓Deep clean weekly (Saturday) — soap + hot water or dishwasher (stainless, top rack).
✓Finch dishes: morning AND evening check. Never fully empty.
✓Lou Dog's water: refresh twice daily minimum. He dunks food constantly.
✗No perches above food or water dishes in any cage.
