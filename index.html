How to build the jar — order matters
1.Kaylor (1½ cups) first — heaviest, goes on the bottom.
2.Freeze-dried chop (10 tbsp) and sprouted seed (4 tbsp).
3.ZuPreem dust (8 tbsp) — pour over the seed so it settles down through.
4.Prairie Blossom (8 tbsp) last — lightest, goes on top.
5.Seal and shake vigorously 20–30 seconds.
How to crush the ZuPreem
Mortar and pestle, zip-lock bag with a rolling pin, or a small spice grinder. Target: fine powder — not visible as a distinct piece. The dust coats the seed so they ingest it without realizing. Crush your full 8 tbsp before mixing into the jar each Sunday.
When Harrison's High Potency Super Fine arrives: swap it in whole (don't crush) at 8 tbsp in place of ZuPreem dust. Leave it as-is — some birds pick it up thinking it's a seed and start eating it over time.

Lou Dog — Green-Cheeked Conure new

Diet overview — how a GCC is different from your other birds
Pellets first A GCC diet is built the opposite way from budgies. Pellets should make up 60–70% of daily intake — not a supplement, the main event. Lou Dog needs to actually eat pellets, not just have them in a mix.
Fresh food daily Vegetables 20–30% of diet. Fruits 10% max — high sugar. This means actual fresh or freeze-dried veg every single day, not optional.
Seed = treat only Seeds are 10% max, offered as treats or in foraging toys. Not a base food. A seed-heavy diet leads directly to malnutrition and a shortened life in conures.
Lifespan commitment A healthy GCC with good diet and care lives 20–30 years. Diet is the single biggest factor in hitting that number.
What to buy — pellets (the most important purchase)
Harrison's High Potency Fine top pick
USDA organic, vet-recommended, sized correctly for a GCC beak. Note: Fine (not Super Fine — that's for budgies). Foil-packaged, 45 days once opened. Buy the 1 lb bag.
Roudybush Daily Maintenance (small) excellent alt
No dyes, no artificial anything, consistent formula. Many GCCs accept it well. Good backup if Lou Dog resists Harrison's.
ZuPreem Natural (medium) acceptable
Not the ZuPreem FruitBlend (dyes, sugar) — the Natural version only. Decent nutrition, some GCCs accept it easily. Use as a transition food if needed.

Whatever pellet Lou Dog comes home eating — keep feeding that first, then transition gradually. A bird that won't eat pellets at all needs the same slow conversion approach as the budgies, but it's more critical here since pellets are the primary food source.

What to buy — supplemental foods
Higgins Sunburst Gourmet (conure/small parrot size)
Occasional foraging mix — fruit, seed, grain pieces. You already have the canary/finch version; get the conure-appropriate size. Used as a treat/foraging food, not a staple.
Lafeber Nutriberries (conure size)
Widely loved by GCCs. Pellet-enriched berry shapes — nutritionally balanced unlike pure seed treats. Great for foraging toys and training rewards. Limit to a few per day.
Freeze-dried fruit/veg chop (same as budgie jar)
Same product you're using for the budgies. Offer daily in a separate fresh food dish alongside pellets.

Never feed ✗

✗Onion and garlic — toxic, breaks down red blood cells.
✗Chocolate and caffeine.

Out-of-cage time — non-negotiable for a GCC
GCCs require a minimum of 2–4 hours of supervised out-of-cage time daily. This isn't enrichment — it's a baseline welfare requirement. A conure kept mostly caged will develop behavioral problems: screaming, feather plucking, biting, and depression. Plan your day around this before Lou Dog comes home.
Bird-safe the room first: no open windows or doors, ceiling fans off, no nonstick cookware in use (Teflon fumes are instantly fatal to birds), no candles or air fresheners, no other pets unsupervised in the room.
Out-of-cage time is also your primary training and bonding window. GCCs are intelligent and love trick training — use Nutriberry pieces or a small nut as rewards. Even 10 minutes of focused training per session makes a big difference.

Treat/egg food dish1 small dish, 3x/week

Total: 5 dishes. GCCs are notorious water-dunkers — they will drop food into every water dish. Two water stations means at least one stays reasonably clean. Change water more frequently than the other birds — at least twice daily.

Coming home — first two weeks
1.Avian vet visit within the first week — same rule as Wyatt. New bird, full health screen before any interaction with other birds.
2.Find out what Lou Dog is currently eating and keep feeding that for the first 1–2 weeks. Moving homes is already stressful — don't change the diet simultaneously.
3.Introduce new foods one at a time after the bird is settled. Offer new items first thing in the morning when hunger is highest.
4.If not already on pellets, begin the same gradual conversion approach as the budgies — new pellets alongside familiar food, increase ratio slowly over weeks.
5.Keep cage separate from budgie cages until cleared by vet and fully settled in — minimum 30 days quarantine from other birds.
⚠ Do NOT use nonstick cookware (Teflon/PTFE) anywhere in the home while Lou Dog is free-roaming. The fumes are colorless, odorless, and kill birds within minutes. This is the #1 accidental cause of parrot death in homes. Switch to stainless, cast iron, or ceramic.
No sleeping huts or fabric tents for Lou Dog. Soft-sided huts cause hormonal behavior and birds can ingest the fibers, causing fatal crop impaction. A rope perch or a designated sleeping spot on a regular perch is the safe alternative.

Maria — Female Cockatiel new

Important: female cockatiel = special health considerations
Chronic egg laying risk Female cockatiels are one of the most prolific chronic egg-layers of any pet bird. Maria can lay infertile eggs solo — no male needed. Each egg drains significant calcium and protein from her body. Without a good diet, this leads to egg binding, brittle bones, seizures, and can be fatal.
Pellets are non-negotiable for her A seed-only diet has no calcium, almost no vitamin A or D3, and no protein adequate for egg production. A female cockatiel on seeds is at serious risk. Pellets are the most important thing you can buy for Maria.
Powder down alert Unlike your other birds, cockatiels are powder-down birds. They produce a fine white dust constantly from specialized feathers. This is normal — but it means more frequent cage cleaning, and anyone with bird allergies will notice it more. A HEPA air purifier near Maria's cage is worth considering.
What to buy — pellets
Harrison's High Potency Fine top pick
Same as Lou Dog — Fine size is right for a cockatiel beak too. USDA organic, vet-recommended. One bag serves both birds.
Roudybush Daily Maintenance (small) excellent alt
Clean formula, no dyes. Good option if Maria resists Harrison's.
Lafeber Nutriberries (cockatiel size) treat + supplement
Pellet-enriched, nutritionally balanced. Great for converting seed-addicted birds — they think it's a treat. Limit 1–2 per day.

Pellets should make up 60–75% of Maria's daily diet. This is higher priority for her than for the budgies because of egg-laying calcium demands.

Safe foods ✓
✓Vegetables: bell pepper (high vitamin A — excellent for cockatiels), broccoli, carrot, kale, collard greens, zucchini, peas, corn, sweet potato, bok choy, dandelion greens.
✓Fruits: apple (no seeds), banana, mango, papaya, blueberry, melon — offered 2–3x/week, small amounts only.
✓Cooked grains: brown rice, quinoa, oats — plain, no seasoning. Good calcium-depleted supplement after egg laying.
✓Cooked egg: scrambled or hard-boiled, plain — especially good after she lays eggs to replenish protein.
✓Calcium sources: cuttlebone (always in cage), dark leafy greens, broccoli. Avoid spinach and Swiss chard — they block calcium absorption.
✓Sprouted seed: same sprouted mix you use for budgies, offered fresh 3–4x/week.

✗Onion and garlic.
✗Chocolate, caffeine.
✗Fruit seeds and pits.

✗Rhubarb, raw beans, xylitol, mushrooms.
✗Raisins.
✗Soy and flax in large amounts — phytoestrogens can trigger egg-laying hormones.
Chronic egg laying — what to watch for and how to prevent it
1.Diet is the #1 prevention. A pellet-fed cockatiel with proper calcium is far less at risk from egg laying complications than a seed-fed one. Get her on Harrison's Fine as the priority.
2.Don't pet her back, wings, or vent area. This stimulates mating behavior and triggers hormonal egg laying. Head and neck only for Maria.
3.Limit daylight to 10–12 hours. Long days trigger reproductive cycles. Cover her cage earlier in the evening than your other birds if needed.
4.No nesting materials. Don't put anything soft, dark, or cave-like in her cage — she will treat it as a nesting spot and begin laying.
5.If she lays an egg: don't remove it immediately. Let her sit on it for 2–3 weeks. Removing it early causes her to replace it with another. Swap the egg for a fake egg if available — she'll eventually abandon it.
6.Egg binding emergency signs: straining, tail bobbing, sitting on the floor, visible distress. This is a same-day vet emergency. Don't wait.
Powder down — what it means for your home
Maria produces a fine white dust from specialized down feathers continuously — it's how cockatiels condition their feathers. It's completely normal and healthy for her. For you it means: cage area needs more frequent wiping down (every 2–3 days rather than weekly), nearby surfaces collect a fine white film, and anyone with respiratory sensitivities or bird allergies will notice it. A HEPA air purifier near her cage makes a significant difference. Never use aerosol sprays, scented candles, air fresheners, or plug-in diffusers near any of your birds — but especially near Maria. Her respiratory system is extremely sensitive.
Coming home — first two weeks
1.Avian vet visit within the first week. For a female cockatiel, the vet will also assess her reproductive health baseline.
2.Keep feeding whatever she came with for 1–2 weeks before introducing Harrison's Fine.
3.30-day quarantine from all other birds — separate cage, separate airspace if possible.
4.Introduce pellets gradually. If she's been on seed, start by crushing pellets into dust and mixing with seed, exactly like the budgie conversion approach.
5.Make sure cuttlebone is in her cage from day one.
⚠ Nonstick cookware fumes (Teflon/PTFE) kill cockatiels just as fast as they kill conures. Maria is equally sensitive. The no-nonstick rule applies to the whole home, not just during Lou Dog's out-of-cage time.

Toys & enrichment by bird

Budgies — Billy, Morgan, Doc, Wyatt
Budgies are curious, social, and love to investigate and chew. They're not as destructive as conures so toys last longer, but they need mental variety more than brute-force chewing. Rotate 2 toys out every Friday to keep things fresh.
🪞 Mirrors
Good for solo birds (Wyatt during integration). Remove once he joins the flock. Watch for obsessive regurgitation at mirror — sign of over-bonding.
🔔 Bells
Budgies love to ring bells. Small stainless steel bells only — no zinc or lead. Remove bell clappers (the small metal piece inside) as they can be chewed off and swallowed.
🪢 Foraging wraps
Paper or coffee filter with a pinch of seed/Prairie Blossom twisted inside. Hang with plain cotton string. Excellent mental enrichment, costs nothing.
🧱 Balsa wood chews
Soft enough for budgie beaks. Keeps beaks trimmed. Small blocks or hanging pieces. Natural, untreated only.
📄 Paper shredders
Toilet paper tube stuffed with paper strips and a little seed. Balsa blocks with paper woven through. Shredding is a natural budgie behavior.
🪜 Swings and ladders
Budgies love swings — they'll use them constantly. Wooden runged ladders give climbing enrichment. Avoid soft rope or fabric that frays into long strands.
🌱 Foraging grass mats
Seagrass woven mats they can climb and pick at. Hide small seeds or freeze-dried pieces in the weave. Great for all four budgies.
🔵 Foot toys
Small corks, wooden beads, plain bottle caps — things they can pick up and carry. Budgies don't hold toys in their feet like parrots do, but they'll push and investigate foot toys on a flat surface.
Finches — Bonnie, Clyde, owl finches, society finches, zebra finches
Finches are not interactive toy players like parrots or budgies. Their enrichment is environmental — variety of perches, things to investigate and land on, and nesting-type materials for the bonded pairs. Keep it simple.
🌿 Natural perches at varied heights
Safe wood branches (willow, apple, birch) at different diameters exercise feet and prevent arthritis. Far more valuable than any toy for finches.
🪺 Nesting material (managed)
Short paper strips (3–4 inches max), dried grass, or plain coconut fiber. Finches will weave and arrange these. Watch Bonnie and Clyde — if they start serious nesting, remove materials to prevent egg-laying cycles. Same for the society finches who are prolific nesters.
🛁 Bath dish
Already in the rotation. Finches treat bathing as a major event — it IS their enrichment. The shower bath on their bath day is the highlight of their week.
🌾 Millet spray clipped to bars
A whole spray millet clipped to the bars gives finches a foraging activity and landing spot in one. Use on treat days within the weekly millet allowance.
❌ What NOT to give finches
No mirrors (can cause aggression in male zebra finches). No jute or long rope strands. No small bells with removable clappers. No hanging toys that swing aggressively — finches startle easily and need stable landing zones.
Lou Dog — Green-Cheeked Conure
GCCs are the most toy-driven birds in your flock. They're intelligent, destructive, and need regular new stimulation. Lou Dog needs more toys than anyone else, rotated more frequently. Expect to replace destroyed toys regularly — that's success, not waste.
🧩 Foraging puzzle toys
Boxes with lids, cups with paper locks, pull-cord feeders. GCCs love problem-solving. Start easy (partially open) and increase difficulty as Lou Dog figures them out. Use Nutriberry pieces or small seeds as the reward inside.
🌿 Seagrass and vine balls
Perfect texture for conure beaks. They'll shred them completely. Sola balls, vine balls, corn husk pieces — natural, safe, destroyable. Stock up because they'll go fast.
🪵 Soft to medium wood chews
Balsa and pine are good starting points. Harder woods like manzanita last longer but are less satisfying to chew. Small scale — toys sized for budgies are too flimsy, toys for Amazons are too big. Conure/cockatiel scale is right.
📄 Shreddable paper toys
Paper rolls, cardboard strips layered on a skewer, newspaper-wrapped treasure bundles. GCCs shred with purpose — give them something to destroy every week.
🔔 Foot toys
Unlike budgies, GCCs actually hold toys in one foot and manipulate them. Small wooden blocks, cork pieces, small leather strips (vegetable-tanned only, no chrome-tanned). Out-of-cage foot toys keep Lou Dog busy during supervised time.
🪢 Boings and rope perches
A boing (coiled rope perch) hung in the middle of the cage is a GCC favorite — they bounce, swing, and sleep on them. Use cotton rope only, inspect regularly for fraying, replace when strands start to unravel.
🎓 Training as enrichment
GCCs are smart enough that 10–15 minutes of trick training per day counts as major enrichment. Step up, wave, spin, target training — use Nutriberry pieces as reward. This is the best enrichment you can give a conure and it builds your bond simultaneously.
Maria — Cockatiel
Cockatiels are somewhere between budgies and conures in play style. They love music, whistling, and social interaction more than destruction. Maria will enjoy toys she can explore and preen rather than demolish.
🔔 Bells
Cockatiels are bell obsessed — they'll ring them and sing at them. Same rule as budgies: stainless steel only, remove clappers.
🪜 Swings
Cockatiels love to swing and perch on moving objects. A swing in the upper area of the cage will get heavy use.
🌾 Millet and foraging toys
Millet spray clipped to bars, small foraging boxes, treat cups. Cockatiels aren't as driven to problem-solve as conures but enjoy simple foraging. Nutriberries work as training treats and enrichment both.
🪵 Light wood chews
Balsa or soft pine pieces. Cockatiels chew less aggressively than conures but enjoy having something to work on. Good for beak health.
🪞 Mirrors — use carefully
Female cockatiels can become bonded to mirrors and treat the reflection as a mate, triggering egg laying. This is bad for Maria specifically. Skip mirrors or use very sparingly and monitor her response closely.
🎵 Music and whistling
Not a toy but the best enrichment for a cockatiel. Maria will likely start to mimic songs, whistles, or household sounds. Play music during the day, whistle to her, and she'll engage enthusiastically. Female cockatiels vocalize less than males but still enjoy sound enrichment.
Universal toy safety rules — all birds
✗No zinc or lead anywhere — galvanized metal, cheap chain links, certain colored dyes. Zinc toxicity is a common and serious bird emergency.
✗No jute rope — frays into long dangerous strands that wrap around toes and necks.
✗No synthetic fibers — polyester, nylon, acrylic. Cotton only for any rope or fabric elements.
✗No sharp edges or points on any plastic or metal pieces.
✗No glue — some toy assemblies use adhesives that are toxic when chewed.
✗No painted or varnished wood — natural and untreated only.
✓Inspect toys weekly — replace anything with fraying rope, broken parts, or sharp edges from chewing damage.
✓Rotate 2 toys per budgie cage every Friday. Lou Dog gets new toys most frequently — weekly or when destroyed.
✓Safe toy materials: untreated natural wood (balsa, pine, willow, birch, apple, manzanita), seagrass, vine, corn husk, palm, unbleached paper, stainless steel, cotton rope, vegetable-tanned leather.

Pellet conversion plan

The recommendation: Harrison's High Potency Super Fine
Why Super Fine, not Fine Fine is sized for cockatiels and small conures. Super Fine is closer to a millet seed — the right scale for a budgie beak. Less intimidating, more likely to be picked up out of curiosity.
Why Harrison's over Roudybush Harrison's Super Fine is already the right size straight from the bag. Roudybush crumbles still need crushing before budgies will accept them. Harrison's is USDA organic, foil-packaged for better freshness, and tends to have a higher acceptance rate with resistant birds.
Shelf life 45 days once opened. Buy the 1 lb bag. Don't bulk buy.
Phase 1 — now, with current ZuPreem
1.Crush ZuPreem to fine dust — measure 8 tbsp of crushed dust into the budgie batch jar.
2.It coats the seed so they ingest it passively with every beak-full.
3.Egg food 3x/week matters more now — it's carrying more nutritional weight without solid pellet coverage.
Phase 2 — when Harrison's Super Fine arrives
1.Measure 8 tbsp of Harrison's Super Fine whole (no crushing) into the batch jar in place of ZuPreem dust.
2.Leave it as-is. Some birds pick it up thinking it's a seed, crack it, and start eating. Don't rush this.
3.If no uptake after 2–3 weeks, offer a small dish of just Harrison's first thing in the morning when they're hungriest. Give the main mix 30 minutes later.
4.Acceptance timeline is weeks to months — completely normal. Keep offering consistently.
No grit — ever. Budgies and finches both crack and hull seeds before swallowing, so grit serves no digestive purpose and can cause fatal crop impaction. Cuttlebone in every cage covers all calcium needs.

Monthly tasks

Do these once a month — pick a consistent day (e.g. 1st Sunday)
1.Nail visual check — all birds. Look for nails that are curling, catching on things, or preventing normal perching. You're looking, not necessarily trimming. Healthy zebra finches rarely need trimming. Budgies typically every 3–6 months.
2.Beak, eyes, and nares check — all birds. Look for: symmetry, smooth texture, clear nostrils, no crust. Flag anything unusual.
3.Scaly face/mite inspection — budgies especially. Look at the cere and beak corners for any white, chalky, or honeycomb-like crusting. Early signs are subtle — compare to photos of healthy budgie ceres if unsure. Finches: check for crusting around eyes and legs.
4.Mite and pest check — cage and food storage. Inspect cage corners, perch joints, and the underside of food storage lids for tiny dark spots, small webs, or any signs of insects. Check the seed jars and batch containers too.
5.Food storage audit. Check batch jar lids are sealing properly. Smell each ingredient bag/jar — seed that smells musty or off should be tossed. Harrison's is 45 days once opened; track the date.
6.Toy inspect and replace. Discard any toys with broken parts, fraying rope, or sharp edges. Replace anything worn.

Every 3–6 months

As needed — schedule when you notice it's time
1.Nail trim — budgies. If nails are catching on fabric, curling, or making perching uncomfortable. Best done by an avian vet or experienced groomer — the quick can bleed badly if cut wrong, especially in dark nails. If doing it yourself, have styptic powder on hand.
2.Wing trim — budgies (optional). A personal choice. Prevents escape and injury during out-of-cage time. Needs to be repeated every few months as flight feathers regrow. Avian vet is the right person for this.
3.Full cage deep clean. Disassemble and scrub the entire cage — bars, tray, grate, perches, toys — with a bird-safe cleaner or diluted white vinegar. Rinse thoroughly and air dry before reassembling.

Annual tasks

Once a year per bird — book ahead, avian vet only
1.Avian vet wellness exam — all birds. Birds hide illness instinctively as a prey survival mechanism. By the time symptoms are visible, they may already be seriously sick. Annual exams catch problems early. The vet will weigh the bird (even small weight changes signal illness), examine beak, nails, eyes, feathers, and listen to breathing. Blood work and fecal analysis may be recommended.
2.Nail and beak trim at the vet — fold this into the annual exam. Cheaper and safer than a separate visit.

Illness — what to watch for daily

Go to the vet promptly if you see any of these
!Fluffed feathers when not sleeping — one of the earliest and most reliable illness signs in all bird species.
!Sitting on the cage floor — birds go to the floor when too weak to perch. Treat as urgent.
!Tail bobbing with each breath — sign of respiratory distress.
!Any clicking, wheezing, or labored breathing — especially in finches, where air sac mites are a known risk.
!Discharge from nostrils or eyes, or crusting around the face/beak.
!Droppings that are watery, discolored, or dramatically changed in volume for more than 1–2 days.
!Not eating or drinking — especially critical for finches, who can decline fast.
!Any unexplained weight loss (birds feel light when you hold them).
NoteBudgies and finches should not be taken to a general dog/cat vet. Find an avian-specific vet or one who is a member of the Association of Avian Veterinarians (AAV).
About feather dust: budgies and zebra finches produce normal dander — not heavy powder. You don't need special protocols. Cockatiels, cockatoos, and African greys are the powder-down species that require extra ventilation and cleaning. Your birds are fine with the regular weekly cage cleaning routine.
